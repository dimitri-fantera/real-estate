# Applying Paul Graham's "How to Get Startup Ideas" to Real Estate

## Core Principles

1. **Don't brainstorm — notice.** Instead of sitting down to "think of startup ideas," pay attention to problems in your daily life. The best ideas come from noticing something broken or missing, not from forced ideation sessions.

2. **Work on the frontier.** Put yourself at the edge of a fast-changing field. People working with new technology (like AI right now) naturally encounter problems others don't see yet. "Live in the future, then build what's missing."

3. **Scratch your own itch.** The strongest founder-market fit comes from solving a problem you personally have. You understand the nuances, you can judge solutions, and you know when it's actually solved.

4. **Look for things that seem broken but everyone tolerates.** People get used to bad workflows. The fact that entire industries run on spreadsheets and nobody questions it is exactly the kind of blind spot Graham describes.

5. **Prefer "well" ideas over "broad" ones.** A small number of people who desperately need something beats a large number who think it's kinda nice. Start narrow and deep.

6. **Turn off your "schlep filter."** People unconsciously avoid ideas that involve tedious, unglamorous work (messy data, regulatory complexity, boring industries). Those are often the best opportunities because competitors avoid them too.

7. **Talk to people.** Ask people what's annoying about their work. Not "would you use X?" but "what do you waste time on?" or "what's the most broken part of your workflow?"

## Where to Look in Real Estate

- **Get close to the problem.** Talk to agents, investors, property managers, lenders, contractors. Ask what they waste time on, what's broken, what they dread doing. Don't pitch — just listen.
- **Work at the frontier.** Real estate is being hit by AI, regulatory changes, interest rate shifts, and generational turnover all at once. That's fertile ground.
- **Follow the schlep.** Real estate is full of tedious, fragmented, legacy processes that nobody wants to fix — title searches, permit tracking, tenant screening, insurance quoting, 1031 exchanges. The messier it is, the better the opportunity.

## What to Look For

- Workflows still done in spreadsheets, email, phone calls, or paper
- Professionals who cobble together 5+ tools to do one job
- Information that's technically public but painful to access (county records, zoning data, permit histories)
- Middlemen who exist purely because a process is opaque or confusing
- Things that are expensive only because they're manual

## Filter Questions

- Do people in real estate already pay to solve this problem (even badly)?
- Is there someone who would say "I would pay for that right now" — not "that's interesting"?
- Is the reason it doesn't exist yet because it's hard/tedious, not because it's impossible?

## Next Step

Spend time with people doing real estate work and notice what's painful, rather than designing a product from your desk.
